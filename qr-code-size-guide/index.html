QR Code Size Guide — What Size Should Your QR Code Be?

The wrong size is the #1 reason QR codes fail to scan. This guide covers minimum dimensions for every use case — business cards, posters, packaging, and screens.

The Universal Minimum: 2 × 2 cm (0.8 × 0.8 inches)

As a general rule, never print a QR code smaller than 2 × 2 cm. Below this size, most phone cameras struggle to resolve the individual modules (the small squares that make up the pattern), especially in anything less than ideal lighting. ISO standard 18004:2015 specifies a minimum QR code module size of 0.264mm for reliable automated scanning — and most real-world deployment guidelines set the whole code minimum at 2cm to ensure that threshold is met in practice.

That said, the "right" size depends on scanning distance. A QR code on a business card is scanned from 15 cm away. A QR code on a billboard is scanned from 10 meters away. The required dimensions scale accordingly. According to the QR code specification, the recommended minimum print size is 2cm × 2cm (0.8 inch) for standard smartphone cameras at typical reading distances.

Quick Reference by Use Case

Business cards: 2.5 × 2.5 cm (1 × 1 inch) minimum. This leaves enough margin around the code and scans reliably at arm's length. Use SVG format for print to avoid pixelation.

Flyers and brochures (A4/Letter): 3 × 3 cm minimum. Readers hold these at about 30 cm distance.

Posters (A3 and larger): 5 × 5 cm minimum. People scan these from 30-60 cm away in hallways and waiting rooms.

Banners and signage (viewed from 1-3 meters): 15 × 15 cm minimum. The formula is roughly: QR code width = scanning distance ÷ 10.

Billboards (viewed from 5+ meters): 50 × 50 cm or larger. At these distances, you need significant size and very high contrast.

Screens and digital displays: At least 240 × 240 pixels at the viewing distance. For websites and emails, 200-300px works for close-range phone scanning.

The Distance Formula

A reliable rule of thumb for any scenario: QR code size = scanning distance ÷ 10. The 10:1 scanning distance rule means a QR code must be 1cm wide for every 10cm of scanning distance. If people will scan from 2 meters away, make the code at least 20 cm wide. If scanning from 30 cm (a menu on a table), at least 3 cm wide. This accounts for typical phone camera capabilities and moderate lighting.

Factors That Affect Minimum Size

Data density: The more data in the QR code, the more modules it contains, and the larger it needs to be for each module to remain scannable. A simple URL like "qrmake.org" produces a much simpler code than a full vCard with 8 fields. If your code looks very dense, increase the size by 20-30%.

Error correction level: Higher error correction (like the Level H that QRMake uses) adds more modules. This makes the code more resilient to damage but also denser. The tradeoff is worth it — especially for printed codes that may get scuffed.

Contrast: Low contrast between the QR code and background requires larger sizing to compensate. Dark code on white background is ideal. Avoid colored codes on colored backgrounds unless you've tested extensively.

Common Sizing Mistakes

Scaling down a complex QR code: If you encode a long URL plus UTM parameters, the code becomes very dense. Shrinking it to fit a small space makes individual modules too small to scan. Shorten the URL first (use a link shortener), then generate the code.

No quiet zone: Every QR code needs a blank margin around it called the "quiet zone" — at least 4 modules wide on all sides. If you crop the QR code right to its edges or place it against a busy background, scanners can't distinguish the code from its surroundings.

Stretching or distorting: QR codes must be perfectly square. Stretching horizontally or vertically breaks the pattern. Always lock the aspect ratio when resizing.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the minimum size for a QR code?

The recommended minimum print size for a QR code is 2cm × 2cm (0.8 inch) for standard smartphone cameras at typical reading distances of 15–30 cm. ISO standard 18004:2015 specifies a minimum QR code module size of 0.264mm for reliable automated scanning. Below 2cm, most phone cameras struggle to resolve individual modules, especially in less-than-ideal lighting.

How big should a QR code be on a business card?

On a business card, print your QR code at minimum 2.5 × 2.5 cm (1 × 1 inch). This is typically scanned from arm's length (about 20–30 cm), so the 2.5cm size comfortably meets the 10:1 scanning distance rule. Use SVG format rather than PNG when printing on business cards to ensure sharp, pixel-perfect edges.

How large does a QR code need to be on a billboard?

For a billboard viewed from 5 meters, your QR code needs to be at least 50 × 50 cm (20 inches). The 10:1 scanning distance rule states that a QR code must be 1cm wide for every 10cm of scanning distance. At 5 meters (500 cm), that means a minimum of 50cm width. At 10 meters, minimum 100cm. Always use SVG format for large-format printing.

Does QR code complexity affect the minimum size?

Yes. The more data encoded in a QR code, the more modules (small squares) it contains, and the larger it must be for each module to remain scannable. A simple URL like "qrmake.org" produces a sparse, easily scannable code. A full vCard with 8 fields produces a dense code that needs 20–30% more size to scan reliably. Always test before printing.

What is the quiet zone around a QR code?

The quiet zone is the mandatory blank white margin surrounding a QR code — at minimum 4 modules wide on all sides. Without this quiet zone, scanners cannot distinguish the code from its surrounding content. Never crop or bleed a QR code to its edges, and never place it against a busy background without adequate white space.

What is the 10:1 scanning distance rule?

The 10:1 scanning distance rule means a QR code must be 1cm wide for every 10cm of scanning distance. For example: scanned from 20cm (business card at arm's length) = minimum 2cm; scanned from 1 meter (poster in a hallway) = minimum 10cm; scanned from 5 meters (billboard) = minimum 50cm. This rule accounts for standard smartphone camera capabilities under typical lighting conditions.
